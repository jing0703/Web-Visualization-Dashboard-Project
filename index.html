Research Interests

We employ genomics, machine learning and eco-evolutionary theories to study microbiomes at the interface of nature, built environment, and humans. The lab’s ultimate goal is to discover the principles underlying the diversity, functioning and resilience of microbial ecosystems and develop tools to enable ecologically informed designs.

Below are several projects that we are actively pursuing:

  • Predicting water microbiome dynamics
  • Eco-evolutionary processes in potable water
  • The role of plumbing-associated pathogens in hospital-acquired infections
  • Development of population-level matrices from personal microbiome data

 

 

 

Predicting water microbiome dynamics

Our recent study discovered that bacteria in tap water rapidly assemble into highly stratified community compositions upon stagnation in residential buildings. Employing an ecological null model and chemical reaction kinetics models further revealed the role of physical characteristics common to modern plumbing designs in shaping the chemical environment and regulating bacterial growth and dispersal.

Motivated by these findings, we are developing and validating models that integrate phylogenetic and functional dimensions of bacterial communities to predict water microbiome dynamics.

 

 

 

Eco-evolutionary processes in potable water

We first view and model the potable water system from an island biogeography perspective, where the city’s water supply is modeled as a mainland community (regional species pool) and water faucets at home as island communities.We seek to develop potable water supplies as a model system to study eco-evolutionary processes with culturing and genomic methods.

 

 

 

The role of plumbing-associated pathogens in hospital-acquired infections

Contact and inhalation of potable water or associated aerosols has been recognized as a route of hospital-acquired infections, yet scarcity in bacterial genomes representing the potable water environment limits infection source-tracking.

We are developing a systematic culture collection of bacterial isolates from potable water supplies and a bacterial whole genome sequencing database.

 

 

 

Development of population-level matrices from personal microbiome data

Sewage is now viewed as a source of public health data that can aid the surveillance of infectious diseases and opioid epidemics.

We are exploring metagenomic datasets from human gut microbiome studies to develop population-level matrices such as population census tools.
